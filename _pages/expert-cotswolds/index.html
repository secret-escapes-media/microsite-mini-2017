Our road trip to the Cotswolds was always going to revolve around cosy pubs and honey-hued villages, and boy, does this part of the country have them by the bucketload. Armed with a brand new MINI Countryman and a map full of pins, the biggest challenge was squeezing everything into one short weekend.

The Old Stocks

After some brief bunny-hopping through central London in rush-hour traffic, Tom thankfully mastered slowing down in an automatic car, and from then on it was a surprisingly swift journey up to Stow-on-the-Wold - right in the heart of the Cotswolds. We arrived at The Old Stocks Inn (a personal favourite of mine) and checked into our stylish ‘Amazing Great Room’ just in time for dinner at the hotel’s buzzy and very popular restaurant.

Dinner began with a ‘high society’ cocktail - this punchy little drink is served in a glass bottle with a stopper and is designed for sharing (it’s so strong that I was already smirking after a thimbleful). This was followed by three sublime courses of fine dining - gin-cured salmon served with a cucumber sorbet, steak and the most scrumptious banoffee dessert. Back in our room, a G&T from the complimentary mini bar and a relaxing soak in the freestanding bath knocked us out before midnight.

We woke up to thick fog and grey skies, but a pea souper wasn’t going to interfere with our packed itinerary.

After stuffing our faces with bacon and maple syrup pancakes, we strolled around Stow - peering through antique shop windows and discovering the two ancient yew trees that flank the doors to St. Edward’s church.

Keen to see as many postcard-perfect villages as possible, we jumped in the MINI Countryman and drove to teeny-tiny Lower Slaughter to fantasise about which fairytale cottage we’d choose to live in.

Bourton-on-the-Water

The next stop was Bourton-on-the-Water, this charming village - known as ‘Venice of the Cotswolds’ because of the small river that runs through it - is positively jam-packed with little boutiques and tea rooms and has many a photo opportunity on the bridges that cross the shallow river.

Lunch was a real treat at the Michelin-starred Wild Rabbit in Kingham - a restaurant I’d been dying to visit since I discovered it via Instagram a while ago. I’m a sucker for rustic-luxe interiors and this dining room is beautiful - all heavy wooden tables and statement greenery. My cod was probably the best fish dish I’ve had in recent memory and Tom felt just as passionately about the lamb.

We’d saved the afternoon for a drive up to Chipping Campden, but we made a brief stop at the Daylesford farm shop - this is a Cotswolds institution for out-of-towners and well-heeled locals. We were far too full to try out the cafe, but I did stock up on jams and then moved on to the homeware department for vases, baskets and candles.

The sun finally made an appearance at about 5pm, just in time to snap some beautiful reflections on the car as we whizzed through the woodland roads on our way to Broadway Tower - a folly at the second-highest point in the Cotswolds. We parked up and power-walked over the muddy grass to the tower - ruining my suede trainers in the process but making it just in time to catch a hazy lilac sunset, with mist still lingering over the hills.

After yet another cracking breakfast at The Old Stocks (baked avocado and poached eggs on sourdough), we set off early to beat the crowds to the much-photographed row of medieval almshouses in Bibury.

The plan worked and we had the place to ourselves, leaving plenty of time to admire a lone black swan that was swimming along the river right next to us - we leant in to take photos up-close, but stayed just far enough away to run for our lives if we needed to...

Blenheim Palace

I love a nosey around other people's houses, so we arrived at Blenheim Palace in Woodstock around lunchtime. We saw the room where Winston Churchill was born during our tour of the building, though my favourite part of the day was a long, wintery walk through the Capability Brown-designed gardens. For two or so hours we wandered past topiary and statues, towering trees and along the deep-green lake, past the boathouse. There are 2000 acres to explore and the gardens alone are well worth a visit.

Another day, another hearty pub dinner.

This time at The Wheatsheaf Inn in Northleach, this fashionable, vine-covered pub played host to Kate Moss's wedding guests and seamlessly mixes modern artwork alongside traditional paintings in its cosy dining room. We warmed our cockles with a glass of Nyetimber by the fire and then tucked into chicken and leek pie, balanced atop a pile of creamy mash. We'd just missed a busy Sunday roast service, but this was the next-best kind of weekend comfort food. We slept soundly on our final night, with full bellies and promises to start diets and join gyms.

The next morning it was time to head back to London, but to make the most of the MINI Countryman's roomy boot space, we may or may not have stopped by Bicester Village (which is near Oxford and very close to the Cotswolds) for some designer shopping and a slice of apple pie at the Soho House-owned Farmshop restaurant. You can take the girl out of the city...
